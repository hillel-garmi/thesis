\title{
  The Galois group of factors of $x^n + ax + b$ for small values of $a,b \in \Z$.
}
\author{Hilel Garmi}
\maketitle
By Hilbert's irreducibility theorem, the Galois group over $\Q$ of almost any polynomial of degree $n$ is $S_n$.
However, elegantly describing infinite families $f_n$ of polynomials of degree $n$ whose Galois group over $\Q$ is $S_n$ for each $n$ remains a difficult problem \editorial{I think, right?}.
One classical example is the family $x^n - x - 1$.
Selmer \cite{SelmerIrreducibility1956} showed that $f\left( x \right) = x^n - x - 1$ is irreducible for each $n$.
Nart and Vila \cite{NartVila1979} were able to show that it's Galois group over $\Q$ is $S_n$.
To achieve that, they first showed that all the inertia groups of $f$ are either trivial or generated by a transposition.
They then utilized Minkowski's bound to prove that the Galois group of $f$ is generated by its inertia groups, and deduced that it is $S_n$ by some well known facts about permutation groups.
It thus makes sense to try to apply a similar strategy for $f\left( x \right) = x^n + ax + b$, or its irreducible factors, for some other values of $a,b \in \Z$.

Nart and Vila \cite{NartVila1979} themselves and Osada \cite{Osada1987TheGG} after them indeed generalized the above method to obtain some sufficient criteria on $a,b$ and $n$ for the Galois group of $f$ to be $S_n$ assuming its irreducibility.
Yet, each of these criteria includes some congruence condition on $n$, leaving out a positive density of the natural numbers.
However, maybe the most istructive example for why these methods fail to generalize to all $n$ is the reducible case of $x^n - 2x + 1$.
It this case $f$ factors as the product of $x-1$ and an the polynomial $g\left( x \right) = x^{n-1} + x^{n-2} + \cdots + x - 1$.
The latter polynomial was shown to be irreducible by Miles \cite{Miles1960} and Wolfram \cite{Wolfram1998} which were actually interested in its close relative, the generalized Fibonacci polynomial $x^{n-1} - x^{n-2} - \cdots - x - 1$.
Martin \cite{MARTIN2004213} succeded, using methods similar to those used in the classical case of $x^n - x - 1$, to show that the Galois group over $\Q$ of $g$ is $S_{n-1}$ for any odd value of $n$.
The reason his proof fails for even values of $n$, is that in this case 
\[
  x^n - 2x + 1 \equiv x^n - 1 \equiv \left( x^{n/2} - 1\right)^2 \mod 2
\]
and thus the inertia group of $f$ over $\Q_2$ is not generated by a transposition anymore.
% Bary-Soroker, Shusterman and Zannier \cite[Appendix A]{Soroker_appendix_2017} proved that for any $n$, the field defined over $\Q$ by $h$ has no totally real subextension.

In this lecture I will present the strategy of a proof that bypasses this problem.
It can be said that the new strategy shifts the focus away from the local behavior of $f$, which gave rise to the congruence conditions on $n$ in the previous works, to a more global approach. 
The new approach makes a heavier use of bounds on the discriminant of number fields, like the mentioned Minkowski's bound and the stronger Odlyzko's bound \cite{OdlyzkoBoundsII}, to show that the "unproblematic" inertia groups, those that are indeed generated by transpositions, already generate the entire Galois group of $f$ over $\Q$, rendering the problematic ones dispensable.
Combining this approach with standard irreducability arguments, for some small values of $a,b \in \Z$, and in particular in any case where $a,b \in \left\{ \pm 1, \pm 2 \right\}$, and for any $n \in \N$, we were able to determine the factorization of $f$ and show that the Galois group of each of its irreducible factor, call it $g$, is indeed $S_{\deg g}$.
